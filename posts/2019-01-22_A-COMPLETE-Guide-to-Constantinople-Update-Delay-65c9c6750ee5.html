What is it all¬†about?

Constantinople is the 2nd part of the Metropolis phase of Ethereum. It was scheduled on 16th Jan‚Äô19 but is postponed due to a new critical bug, which would appear on the update. We will talk more about this below(in EIP-1283).

Constantinople Update is work towards improving the performance of the ethereum network. It also delays the difficulty bomb and reduces miner rewards.

Why is it Important?

In the last year, ethereum started facing a lot of competition from a few other platforms like, EOS, Stellar, Cardano, NEM and NEO, due to their better throughput(though some of them lack the smart contract platform as good as ethereum). So, this update mainly focuses on improving the throughput and usability of the network.

What‚Äôs the deal with BILLION transaction/second?

Those 1 billion tps aren‚Äôt generalized transactions. If you do 1 thing 1000 times per second, we can do it very efficiently with state channels. But with how EIP-1014 will optimize state channels, we should see some second-layer solutions starting to come into play (think OmiseGo, Loom Network, Raiden, etc.)

Developer‚Äôs point of¬†view

This update introduces 5 new EIPs.

First 4 EIPs bring improvements in EVM and thus make it cheaper for people to run their smart contracts on the network.

  • EIP-145: This adds bitwise-shifting to the EVM inherently. Bitwise shifting is the process of moving the bits of a number to the left or right. With these instructions, these operations will use 10 times less gas, which means running SOME(not all) smart contracts will be cheaper.
  • EIP-1052: This introduces a new operation that allows smart contracts to verify the code of another contract more efficiently. Before its introduction, this verification required you to grab the code of another contract and verifying it. This new operation returns a hash of the code of the contract, allowing you to check that instead.
  • EIP-1014: This will add state channels to Ethereum. This(state channel) allows certain transactions to be settled away from the blockchain. This can be compared to Bitcoin‚Äôs Lighting network. This could greatly improve the throughput of the ethereum network.

What the heck is a difficulty bomb?

EIP-1234 wants to delay the difficulty bomb. So what‚Äôs that?

Well, it all has to do with Ethereum‚Äôs plans to move away from the proof-of-work(PoW) algorithm and adopt proof-of-stake(PoS).

Right now miners are needed to process transactions and create new blocks(PoW). As compensation for their hard work, they receive the block reward. With proof-of-stake however, miners become obsolete, meaning that they‚Äôll lose their revenue. So when Ethereum finally switches to proof-of-stake, miners can choose to not adopt the update and instead keep the old version running. It would be a true hard fork where the community is torn apart, each going their own direction. This happened to Ethereum in the past when it was forked into Ethereum Classic. The developers don‚Äôt want it to happen again and so they added the difficulty bomb into the protocol. It‚Äôs a special mechanism that makes mining a new block exponentially more difficult over time, until at some point it becomes impossible, effectively freezing the entire network. This is also called the Ethereum Ice Age and it was first added on September 7th, 2015. The difficulty bomb accomplishes two things:

  • First, it puts pressure on the developers of Ethereum to come up with an update for the protocol because they obviously don‚Äôt want it to go into an ice age.
  • And secondly, it forces miners to adopt new updates because otherwise, they‚Äôll end up with a blockchain that can‚Äôt make them any money. In other words, they can‚Äôt stick with a network that is increasingly becoming slower.

The difficulty bomb is a great idea, but not perfect. After the original introduction in 2015, the plan was to make the network almost unusable by the end of 2016. The idea was that the switch to proof-of-stake would‚Äôve been made by that time. But obviously, that didn‚Äôt happen. Developers don‚Äôt want to rush the Casper update, the one where they will adopt proof-of-stake. They want to make sure that the new protocol is as safe as the old one. So the difficulty bomb has been delayed a few times, including now with the Constantinople update. It‚Äôs still there waiting to put pressure on developers and miners, but for now, its defused, giving everyone more time.

Why would we want to Reduce the Block¬†Reward?

Up until now, miners on the Ethereum network get 3 ether when they mine a new block. This is called the block reward. A new block is created roughly every 15 seconds meaning that every day there are 5000 to 6000 new blocks added. With a block reward of 3 ether, every day approximately 15,000 to 18,000 new ether gets added to the total supply. This can become a big issue because the ever-growing supply of ether puts the price under pressure(inflation).

You see, if something is scarce, it increases in value but if something is available in abundance, it decreases. In the case of a cryptocurrency: it can push people towards selling their coins because they decrease in value. So, reducing the issuance of new coins will effectively reduce the inflation of ether and will probably drive the price of ether up. It will also attract new investors because they know that the value will be more stable. So reducing the block reward makes sense. Bitcoin has a similar, but completely automated, mechanism where the issuance is halved after every 210,000th block, again trying to make the resource more scarce. Some people suggested reducing the issuance even further, to 1 ether. While it would likely push the price of ether even higher, miners would earn a lot less. And that‚Äôs a potential issue because the network needs miners to secure the platform. If a lot of miners stop mining Ethereum it could weaken the network and make it vulnerable to attacks. So instead, a more conservative reduction in block reward was chosen in the hope that most miners will stick around.
